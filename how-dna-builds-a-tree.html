How DNA Builds a Tree

From Specimen to Sequence

The raw material for phylogenetic analysis is DNA extracted from fish tissue — typically a small fin clip taken from a live or freshly preserved specimen. The tissue is homogenized and processed through a standard extraction protocol that lyses cells, removes proteins and lipids, and precipitates pure genomic DNA. From that extract, a pair of universal PCR primers flanking the COI barcode region amplifies the target segment. The PCR product is then sent for Sanger sequencing — the gold standard method for generating individual sequences. Sanger sequencing works by incorporating fluorescently labeled chain-terminating nucleotides during DNA synthesis, producing fragments of every possible length. When separated by capillary electrophoresis and read by a laser detector, these fragments produce a chromatogram: a colored peak trace where each color represents one of the four bases. Software reads the peaks and outputs a string of A, C, G, and T characters approximately 650 base pairs long. That string — one sequence per specimen — is deposited in GenBank and becomes the permanent public record of that individual's COI barcode. All sequences in this study were downloaded from GenBank rather than generated in the lab; the accession numbers are listed on the Build Your Own Tree page.

Alignment and Matrix Construction

Before sequences can be compared, they must be aligned. Raw COI sequences from different individuals are slightly different lengths and may have small insertions or deletions relative to one another. Alignment inserts gap characters so that column n in the final matrix represents the same homologous nucleotide position across all sequences — meaning every base in a column evolved from the same ancestral site. The result is a rectangular matrix where rows are taxa and columns are aligned nucleotide positions. This matrix is the direct input to all three tree-building methods used in this study. In this project, MUSCLE was used for alignment, producing a matrix of approximately 660 columns across 24 sequences representing 8 species.

Inferring the Tree

Three complementary methods were used to infer the evolutionary tree from the aligned matrix, and their agreement provides confidence in the result. Neighbor-Joining (NJ) compresses the alignment into a matrix of pairwise distances and finds the tree that minimizes total distance — it is extremely fast and gives a reliable first approximation. Maximum Likelihood (ML) instead evaluates the full alignment under an explicit probabilistic model of DNA evolution, finding the tree that makes the observed sequence data most probable under that model; it is more computationally intensive but generally more accurate. Bayesian inference uses Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to estimate a probability distribution over all possible trees, returning posterior probabilities rather than point estimates. When all three methods agree on a grouping, confidence in that grouping is high; when they disagree, it signals a region of tree space that the data cannot resolve with a single barcode gene.

Reading a Phylogeny

A phylogenetic tree has three types of elements. Tips (also called leaves or terminal nodes) are the taxa in the study. Internal nodes represent hypothetical common ancestors: the point in the past where two lineages diverged. Branch lengths represent evolutionary distance, measured in substitutions per site under the model used, so longer branches mean more DNA change accumulated along that lineage. The root is the oldest node in the tree and gives it direction — without an outgroup to place the root, a tree is unrooted and does not imply ancestor- descendant relationships.

In this study, Bull Shark serves as the outgroup: it diverged from all bony fish roughly 420 million years ago, far deeper than any split within the ingroup, which places the root unambiguously at the base of the tree. A node labeled with 100% bootstrap support means that identical grouping appeared in all 1,000 bootstrap replicates — very strong evidence that the clade is real and not an artifact of the particular sequences sampled.

What is an outgroup and why does it matter?

An outgroup is a taxon known to have diverged from all members of the study group before any of them diverged from each other. Including an outgroup allows the tree to be rooted — given a direction in time. Without an outgroup, the tree is unrooted: it shows topology (who is related to whom) but not polarity (which lineage is ancestral). A good outgroup should be clearly outside the ingroup but not so distantly related that sequence similarity breaks down and alignment becomes unreliable. In this study, Bull Shark (Carcharhinus leucas) is the primary outgroup: as an elasmobranch, it diverged from all bony ray-finned fishes roughly 420 million years ago, unambiguously placing it at the base of the tree. Flathead Mullet is used as an alternative outgroup in sensitivity analyses, and the interactive outgroup explorer on the Explore page lets you re-root the NJ tree on any of the ten sequenced species to see how outgroup choice affects the display of internal relationships.

How Neighbor-Joining works

Neighbor-Joining (NJ) is a distance-based method. It first compresses the full sequence alignment into a single pairwise distance matrix — in this project using the Kimura 2-parameter (K80) model, which corrects for the different rates of transitions (A↔G, C↔T) and transversions (A↔C, A↔T, G↔C, G↔T) that are typical of mitochondrial DNA. The algorithm then iteratively picks the pair of taxa with the shortest corrected distance and joins them at a new internal node, updating all remaining distances to that node. This continues until only two lineages remain and the tree is complete. NJ is extremely fast — it runs in seconds on a laptop for datasets of hundreds of taxa — and almost always recovers the correct major groupings when sequences diverge by less than about 20%. Its weakness is that it discards all column-by-column information once the distance matrix is computed, so it cannot take advantage of which specific positions changed. Confidence is assessed by bootstrap resampling: columns of the alignment are sampled with replacement 1,000 times and a fresh NJ tree is built each time; the bootstrap percentage on each node is the fraction of replicates in which that clade appears.

How Maximum Likelihood works

Maximum Likelihood (ML) treats tree-building as a statistical estimation problem. It defines an explicit probabilistic model of DNA evolution — here GTR+Γ+I, which allows all six substitution rates to differ, models rate variation across sites with a gamma distribution, and allows a fraction of sites to be invariant — and then asks: given this model, what tree topology and branch lengths make the observed alignment most probable? The likelihood of each column is computed independently and multiplied together across the alignment (or summed in log space), so every variable position contributes. The optimization searches tree space by rearranging branches with nearest-neighbor interchange and subtree pruning/regrafting moves, accepting changes that raise the likelihood. ML is far more computationally intensive than NJ but generally more accurate because it uses the full alignment rather than a distance summary. Bootstrap support is calculated by running the full ML search on each of 1,000 resampled alignments.

How Bayesian Inference works

Bayesian inference (BI) uses the same likelihood model as ML but frames the problem differently: instead of finding the single best tree, it estimates the probability distribution over all possible trees given the data. In practice, this is done with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC): a chain of random tree proposals is run for millions of steps, with each proposal accepted or rejected according to how much it raises or lowers the posterior probability. After a "burn-in" period during which the chain converges, the sampled trees are summarized into a majority-rule consensus tree, and the proportion of samples containing each clade becomes that clade's posterior probability. A posterior probability of 0.95 means that 95% of sampled trees contained that grouping — a direct probabilistic statement about the clade's support. This is conceptually different from a bootstrap percentage (which measures how often a grouping reappears under resampling of the data) but in practice similarly high values (>0.95 PP or >95% bootstrap) both indicate strong, reliable support. MrBayes was used for the Bayesian analysis in this study, running two independent chains for 2 million generations.

Comparing the three methods

The three methods make different assumptions and use the sequence data in different ways, so their agreement provides independent lines of evidence. NJ is the fastest and most transparent: you can inspect the distance matrix and understand exactly why two taxa are joined. ML and Bayesian inference both use the full alignment and an explicit substitution model, making them more sensitive to subtle signal in the data but also more sensitive to model misspecification. In practice, all three methods agree on the major groupings in this dataset — the family-level clades (Sciaenidae, Sparidae, Paralichthyidae, Mugilidae, Carcharhinidae, Salmonidae) are recovered at 100% bootstrap support or posterior probability ≥ 0.99 by every method — which is strong evidence that these groupings reflect real evolutionary history rather than analytical artifact. Where the methods give different answers (typically in weakly supported internal nodes), that disagreement is informative: it flags regions of the tree where the COI barcode alone does not contain enough signal to resolve the relationship, and where additional loci or longer sequences would be needed.
