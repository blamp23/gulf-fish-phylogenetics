How DNA Builds a Tree

From Specimen to Sequence

The raw material for phylogenetic analysis is DNA extracted from fish tissue — typically a small fin clip taken from a live or freshly preserved specimen. The tissue is homogenized and processed through a standard extraction protocol that lyses cells, removes proteins and lipids, and precipitates pure genomic DNA. From that extract, a pair of universal PCR primers flanking the COI barcode region1 amplifies the target segment. The PCR product is then sent for Sanger sequencing — the gold standard method for generating individual sequences. Sanger sequencing works by incorporating fluorescently labeled chain-terminating nucleotides during DNA synthesis, producing fragments of every possible length. When separated by capillary electrophoresis and read by a laser detector, these fragments produce a chromatogram: a colored peak trace where each color represents one of the four bases. Software reads the peaks and outputs a string of A, C, G, and T characters approximately 650 base pairs long. That string — one sequence per specimen — is deposited in GenBank and becomes the permanent public record of that individual's COI barcode. All sequences in this study were downloaded from GenBank rather than generated in the lab; the accession numbers are listed on the Build Your Own Tree page.

Sanger sequencing produces ~650 bp of COI. A fish genome is roughly 800 million bp. How can 0.00007% of the genome reliably distinguish species?

Two factors make a small gene fragment sufficient. First, the COI region has evolved at a rate that creates consistent, measurable differences between species (typically 2–15% divergence) while staying nearly identical within species (usually <1%). This rate — not too fast, not too slow — is what makes it a reliable marker. Second, species boundaries are real biological entities: once two populations stop interbreeding, their genomes begin accumulating mutations independently. After millions of generations of separation, even a small window into one gene captures that accumulated divergence. The key insight is that you're not trying to describe the whole genome — you're exploiting the predictable rate of one mitochondrial gene as a clock that ticks differently within and between species.

All sequences in this study were downloaded from GenBank rather than generated in the lab. What quality concerns does this introduce, and how does using two or three sequences per species help address them?

GenBank is publicly submitted, meaning sequences can be mislabeled, contaminated, or derived from misidentified voucher specimens. A single downloaded sequence has no internal check on these potential errors. Using two or three sequences per species provides a built-in consistency check: if all replicates for a species cluster tightly together on the tree (within-species distances <1%), the sequences are almost certainly from the same organism and correctly labeled. If one sequence sits far from its stated siblings, that is a red flag — either the sequence was mislabeled in GenBank, the voucher specimen was misidentified, or there was contamination during the original study. In our dataset, every within- species triplet clustered perfectly, giving confidence in the data quality.

Alignment and Matrix Construction

Before sequences can be compared, they must be aligned. Raw COI sequences from different individuals are slightly different lengths and may have small insertions or deletions relative to one another. Alignment inserts gap characters so that column n in the final matrix represents the same homologous nucleotide position across all sequences — meaning every base in a column evolved from the same ancestral site. The result is a rectangular matrix where rows are taxa and columns are aligned nucleotide positions. This matrix is the direct input to all three tree-building methods used in this study. In this project, MUSCLE8 was used for alignment, producing a matrix of approximately 660 columns across 24 sequences representing 8 species.

The alignment viewer shows a nearly solid block of conserved color in the first ~50 columns, then more variation. What does that conserved block tell you about that region of COI, and why would primers be designed to flank it?

A conserved block means every sequence in the dataset shares nearly the same nucleotide at those positions — the site has been under strong purifying selection across all these lineages for hundreds of millions of years. PCR primers anneal most reliably where sequence is conserved, because a primer designed against a conserved region will bind to Bull Shark, Red Drum, and Rainbow Trout alike using the same primer pair. If the primer target region were variable, the primer would fail to bind in some species. The standard COI barcode primers (LCO1490 and HCO2198) were designed to flank the conserved flanks of the variable barcode window — a setup that lets a single universal primer pair amplify the barcode from essentially any animal species.

A classmate argues that aligning sequences before building the tree is an arbitrary step that could introduce error. How would you respond?

Alignment is not arbitrary — it is mandatory. Distance calculations and likelihood functions both assume that column n in the matrix represents the same homologous position in every sequence (i.e., descended from the same ancestral nucleotide). Without alignment, you would be comparing random positions, and the resulting distances would measure nothing biologically meaningful. The potential for alignment error is real — if two sequences are very divergent, automated aligners can occasionally misplace gaps — but the solution is to inspect the alignment (as you can do with the viewer above) and manually correct obvious errors, not to skip alignment entirely. For COI barcodes from reasonably related species, MUSCLE alignment is highly reliable and rarely introduces errors.

Inferring the Tree

Three complementary methods were used to infer the evolutionary tree from the aligned matrix, and their agreement provides confidence in the result. Neighbor-Joining (NJ)4 compresses the alignment into a matrix of pairwise distances and finds the tree that minimizes total distance — it is extremely fast and gives a reliable first approximation. Maximum Likelihood (ML)5 instead evaluates the full alignment under an explicit probabilistic model of DNA evolution, finding the tree that makes the observed sequence data most probable under that model; it is more computationally intensive but generally more accurate. Bayesian inference7 uses Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to estimate a probability distribution over all possible trees, returning posterior probabilities rather than point estimates. When all three methods agree on a grouping, confidence in that grouping is high; when they disagree, it signals a region of tree space that the data cannot resolve with a single barcode gene.

You run all three methods and get trees that agree on family-level groupings but disagree on two internal nodes within Sciaenidae — NJ shows 38% bootstrap, ML shows 29%, Bayesian shows PP = 0.54 at those nodes. What should you conclude?

Consistent low support across three independent methods is actually informative and reassuring: the data are simply insufficient to resolve those two specific branching events, and all three methods are honestly reporting that uncertainty. This is a biological result, not a methodological failure. A single 650 bp gene contains limited phylogenetic signal for distinguishing very recent divergences among closely related species. The correct interpretation is that the family grouping is strongly supported (100% / PP = 1.00 at the node uniting all Sciaenidae), but the exact order in which the four family members diverged from each other cannot be determined from COI alone. The appropriate next step is to add more data — either additional loci or a whole-genome approach — not to choose whichever tree topology looks most convenient.

If NJ and ML give identical topologies but Bayesian inference places one species in a different position with PP = 0.62, which tree should you report and why?

Report all three trees and describe the disagreement explicitly. The Bayesian placement at PP = 0.62 means there is meaningful probability mass on an alternative arrangement — the data support both placements to some degree. Hiding the disagreement by reporting only the NJ/ML consensus would misrepresent the certainty of the result. In practice, a figure can show the majority-rule topology with a note that one node differs in the Bayesian analysis, or all three trees can be shown side by side. The disagreement itself is a finding: it identifies a node in the tree where COI alone cannot resolve relationships, and where additional data would be most valuable.

Reading a Phylogeny

A phylogenetic tree has three types of elements. Tips (also called leaves or terminal nodes) are the taxa in the study. Internal nodes represent hypothetical common ancestors: the point in the past where two lineages diverged. Branch lengths represent evolutionary distance, measured in substitutions per site under the model used, so longer branches mean more DNA change accumulated along that lineage. The root is the oldest node in the tree and gives it direction — without an outgroup to place the root, a tree is unrooted and does not imply ancestor- descendant relationships.

In this study, Bull Shark serves as the outgroup: it diverged from all bony fish roughly 420 million years ago, far deeper than any split within the ingroup, which places the root unambiguously at the base of the tree. A node labeled with 100% bootstrap10 support means that identical grouping appeared in all 1,000 bootstrap replicates — very strong evidence that the clade is real and not an artifact of the particular sequences sampled.

What is an outgroup and why does it matter?

An outgroup is a taxon known to have diverged from all members of the study group before any of them diverged from each other. Including an outgroup allows the tree to be rooted — given a direction in time. Without an outgroup, the tree is unrooted: it shows topology (who is related to whom) but not polarity (which lineage is ancestral). A good outgroup should be clearly outside the ingroup but not so distantly related that sequence similarity breaks down and alignment becomes unreliable. In this study, Bull Shark (Carcharhinus leucas) is the primary outgroup: as an elasmobranch, it diverged from all bony ray-finned fishes roughly 420 million years ago, unambiguously placing it at the base of the tree. Flathead Mullet is used as an alternative outgroup in sensitivity analyses, and the interactive outgroup explorer on the Explore page lets you re-root the NJ tree on any of the ten sequenced species to see how outgroup choice affects the display of internal relationships.

If you rooted this study's tree on Speckled Trout instead of Bull Shark, what would change about the tree — and what would stay the same?

The topology (the branching pattern — who is related to whom) is an intrinsic property of the data and does not change when you move the root. What changes is the direction of the tree: the root determines which side of each split is "older." Placing the root on Speckled Trout would force the Sciaenidae family to appear paraphyletic — split by the root — even though the actual evolutionary relationships among the drums haven't changed at all. This is why outgroup choice matters: a poorly chosen outgroup doesn't alter the relationships in the ingroup, but it can produce a tree display that looks dramatically different and is much harder to interpret correctly. The interactive outgroup explorer on the Explore page lets you see exactly this effect firsthand.

How Neighbor-Joining works

Neighbor-Joining (NJ) is a distance-based method. It first compresses the full sequence alignment into a single pairwise distance matrix — in this project using the Kimura 2-parameter (K80) model3, which corrects for the different rates of transitions (A↔G, C↔T) and transversions (A↔C, A↔T, G↔C, G↔T) that are typical of mitochondrial DNA. The algorithm then iteratively picks the pair of taxa with the shortest corrected distance and joins them at a new internal node, updating all remaining distances to that node. This continues until only two lineages remain and the tree is complete. NJ is extremely fast — it runs in seconds on a laptop for datasets of hundreds of taxa — and almost always recovers the correct major groupings when sequences diverge by less than about 20%. Its weakness is that it discards all column-by-column information once the distance matrix is computed, so it cannot take advantage of which specific positions changed. Confidence is assessed by bootstrap resampling: columns of the alignment are sampled with replacement 1,000 times and a fresh NJ tree is built each time; the bootstrap percentage on each node is the fraction of replicates in which that clade appears.

NJ compresses the alignment into a distance matrix before building the tree, discarding all column-by-column information. Give a concrete example of a scenario where this data compression could lead NJ to an incorrect grouping that ML would get right.

Consider two distantly related species that happen to have converged on the same nucleotide at many variable positions — not because they share a common ancestor that had that base, but because the same mutation arose independently in both lineages. In the distance matrix, their pairwise distance will be artificially small (they look similar overall), and NJ may incorrectly group them together. ML, by contrast, evaluates each column individually under a model that accounts for the probability of independent convergence — it can detect that the pattern of similarities doesn't fit a shared-ancestry explanation as well as it fits a convergence explanation, and place the taxa correctly. This is called long-branch attraction when it involves rapidly evolving lineages, and it is one of NJ's known failure modes that model-based methods like ML handle better.

How Maximum Likelihood works

Maximum Likelihood (ML) treats tree-building as a statistical estimation problem. It defines an explicit probabilistic model of DNA evolution — here GTR+Γ+I6, which allows all six substitution rates to differ, models rate variation across sites with a gamma distribution, and allows a fraction of sites to be invariant — and then asks: given this model, what tree topology and branch lengths make the observed alignment most probable? The likelihood of each column is computed independently and multiplied together across the alignment (or summed in log space), so every variable position contributes. The optimization searches tree space by rearranging branches with nearest-neighbor interchange and subtree pruning/regrafting moves, accepting changes that raise the likelihood. ML is far more computationally intensive than NJ but generally more accurate because it uses the full alignment rather than a distance summary. Bootstrap support is calculated by running the full ML search on each of 1,000 resampled alignments.

GTR+Γ+I is the most parameter-rich of the standard substitution models. Why not always use the simplest model (e.g., JC, which assumes all substitution rates are equal), and why not always use the most complex model available?

The simplest model (Jukes-Cantor, JC) assumes all four bases are equally frequent and all substitution rates are equal — neither of which is true for COI, where transitions are far more common than transversions and base composition is skewed. Using an underparameterized model produces biased distance estimates and can distort topology. But adding parameters beyond what the data can support introduces a different problem: overfitting. A highly complex model with more parameters than the data can constrain produces unstable estimates with large uncertainty, and can actually decrease phylogenetic accuracy. The solution is model selection — tools like AIC or BIC penalize models for the number of parameters they use, rewarding better fit only if the improvement outweighs the added complexity. In practice for mitochondrial barcodes, GTR+Γ+I is almost always the justified choice: complex enough to capture COI's real evolutionary dynamics, standard enough to be reproducible and comparable across studies.

How Bayesian Inference works

Bayesian inference (BI)7 uses the same likelihood model as ML but frames the problem differently: instead of finding the single best tree, it estimates the probability distribution over all possible trees given the data. In practice, this is done with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC): a chain of random tree proposals is run for millions of steps, with each proposal accepted or rejected according to how much it raises or lowers the posterior probability. After a "burn-in" period during which the chain converges, the sampled trees are summarized into a majority-rule consensus tree, and the proportion of samples containing each clade becomes that clade's posterior probability. A posterior probability of 0.95 means that 95% of sampled trees contained that grouping — a direct probabilistic statement about the clade's support. This is conceptually different from a bootstrap percentage (which measures how often a grouping reappears under resampling of the data) but in practice similarly high values (>0.95 PP or >95% bootstrap) both indicate strong, reliable support. MrBayes was used for the Bayesian analysis in this study, running two independent chains for 2 million generations.

A Bayesian run returns PP = 1.00 at a node. A critic argues this means the method is overconfident and the result can't be trusted. How would you respond?

PP = 1.00 (often displayed as 1.00 rounded from, say, 0.9998) means that every sampled post-burn-in tree in the MCMC run contained that clade — not a single sampled alternative was found. For a well-mixed, converged chain with high ESS values, this is a legitimate result: the data so strongly favor that grouping that the chain never wandered to an alternative. The critic's concern would be valid if the MCMC hadn't converged — if the chain was stuck in one region of tree space and hadn't explored alternatives. That's why convergence diagnostics (Tracer, checking ESS, running multiple independent chains) are essential before trusting high PP values. Assuming convergence is confirmed, PP = 1.00 is not overconfidence — it is the correct summary of what the posterior distribution actually looks like for strong phylogenetic signal.

Comparing the three methods

The three methods make different assumptions and use the sequence data in different ways, so their agreement provides independent lines of evidence. NJ is the fastest and most transparent: you can inspect the distance matrix and understand exactly why two taxa are joined. ML and Bayesian inference both use the full alignment and an explicit substitution model, making them more sensitive to subtle signal in the data but also more sensitive to model misspecification. In practice, all three methods agree on the major groupings in this dataset — the family-level clades (Sciaenidae, Sparidae, Paralichthyidae, Mugilidae, Carcharhinidae, Salmonidae) are recovered at 100% bootstrap support or posterior probability ≥ 0.99 by every method — which is strong evidence that these groupings reflect real evolutionary history rather than analytical artifact. Where the methods give different answers (typically in weakly supported internal nodes), that disagreement is informative: it flags regions of the tree where the COI barcode alone does not contain enough signal to resolve the relationship, and where additional loci or longer sequences would be needed.

A reviewer asks you to justify why you ran all three methods instead of just reporting the ML tree, which is generally considered the most accurate. What is your response?

Each method provides an independent line of evidence from different mathematical frameworks, and their agreement or disagreement is itself informative. NJ gives a transparent, distance-based view that is easy to explain and replicate; if NJ and ML agree, the result is unlikely to be a model artifact. Bayesian inference provides posterior probabilities — a fundamentally different kind of support measure that answers a different question than bootstrap resampling. When all three methods agree on a grouping at high support, confidence in that grouping is higher than any single method alone can provide, because you have ruled out artifacts specific to any one approach. Disagreements are equally valuable: a node where NJ and ML agree but Bayesian PP is moderate identifies a region of uncertainty that the data genuinely cannot resolve, and that signal would be invisible if only one method were reported.
