How DNA Builds a Tree

From Specimen to Sequence

The raw material for this analysis is DNA extracted from fish tissue — typically a small fin clip taken from a live or freshly preserved specimen. The tissue is homogenized and processed through a standard extraction protocol that lyses cells, removes proteins and lipids, and precipitates pure DNA. From that extract, a universal primer pair designed to flank the COI barcode region amplifies the target stretch using the polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The PCR product — a segment roughly 650 base pairs long — is then sequenced using Sanger sequencing, which generates a chromatogram: a colored peak trace where each color represents one of the four nucleotide bases. Software reads the peaks and outputs a string of A, C, G, and T characters. That string is the raw sequence and the starting point for everything that follows.

Alignment and Matrix Construction

Before sequences can be compared, they must be aligned. Raw COI sequences from different individuals are slightly different lengths and may have small insertions or deletions relative to one another. Alignment inserts gap characters so that column n in the final matrix represents the same homologous nucleotide position across all sequences — meaning every base in a column evolved from the same ancestral site. The result is a rectangular matrix where rows are taxa and columns are aligned nucleotide positions. This matrix is the direct input to all three tree-building methods used in this study. In this project, MUSCLE was used for alignment, producing a matrix of approximately 660 columns across 24 sequences representing 8 Gulf species.

Inferring the Tree

Three complementary methods were used to infer the evolutionary tree from the aligned matrix, and their agreement provides confidence in the result. Neighbor-Joining (NJ) compresses the alignment into a matrix of pairwise distances and finds the tree that minimizes total distance — it is extremely fast and gives a reliable first approximation. Maximum Likelihood (ML) instead evaluates the full alignment under an explicit probabilistic model of DNA evolution, finding the tree that makes the observed sequence data most probable under that model; it is more computationally intensive but generally more accurate. Bayesian inference uses Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to estimate a probability distribution over all possible trees, returning posterior probabilities rather than point estimates. When all three methods agree on a grouping, confidence in that grouping is high; when they disagree, it signals a region of tree space that the data cannot resolve with a single barcode gene.

Reading a Phylogeny

A phylogenetic tree has three types of elements. Tips (also called leaves or terminal nodes) are the taxa in the study — in this case individual COI sequences labeled by species. Internal nodes represent hypothetical common ancestors: the point in the past where two lineages diverged from a shared ancestor. Branch lengths represent evolutionary distance, measured in substitutions per site under the model used, so longer branches mean more DNA change accumulated along that lineage. The root is the oldest node in the tree and gives it direction — without an outgroup to place the root, a tree is unrooted and does not imply ancestor- descendant relationships. In this study, Bull Shark serves as the outgroup: it diverged from all bony fish roughly 420 million years ago, far deeper than any split within the ingroup, which places the root unambiguously at the base of the tree. A node labeled with 100% bootstrap support means that identical grouping appeared in all 1,000 bootstrap replicates — very strong evidence that the clade is real and not an artifact of the particular sequences sampled.
